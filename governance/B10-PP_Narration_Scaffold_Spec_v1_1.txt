1. PURPOSE AND SCOPE

This document specifies the scaffold configuration system for past tense narration
tasks in the B10 Practice Platform (B10-PP). The scaffold allows instructors to
configure AI scoring and feedback focus for individual task assignments targeting
ILR 1 to ILR 2 speaking proficiency.

Scope: Past tense narration tasks only. ILR 2 is the performance target. ILR 1 is
the assumed floor. ILR 3 constructs (argumentation, hedging, abstract evaluation)
are explicitly out of scope.

================================================================================

2. DESIGN PRINCIPLES

- Fixed infrastructure and instructor-configurable focus are strictly separated.
  Construct-critical rules are never instructor-modifiable.

- Primary focus drives feedback priority. The scorer leads with the
  instructor-selected dimension.

- A monitor is a performance feature that the scoring engine tracks and reports on
  in feedback output. Monitors do not affect scoring, do not modify the gate
  criterion, and do not determine the primary feedback focus selected by the
  instructor. They surface targeted observational data alongside the primary focus
  feedback. Instructors use monitors to request visibility into specific dimensions
  without shifting the main feedback target.

- Past tense gate is always active and never configurable. Selecting Past Tense
  Control as a primary focus affects feedback emphasis only — not scoring logic.

- Reference clarity is observer-visible but not learner-actionable as a standalone
  practice target. It lives in the monitor layer only and is never a primary focus
  option.

- Lexical range is managed through topic domain selection at task design, not
  through a scaffold focus category.

- Basic Structures monitoring includes only forms that directly support
  intelligibility at ILR 1-2. Features excluded from this monitor — article errors,
  preposition errors, complex clause errors — do not reliably impede comprehension
  at this level and are treated as ILR 3+ precision concerns outside the scope of
  this instrument.

- Disfluency monitoring does not change the scoring cap rule. It shifts feedback
  priority only.

================================================================================

3. REDS FRAMEWORK AND SCORING ALIGNMENT

REDS FRAMEWORK — FUNCTIONAL PERFORMANCE STATES

Performance is evaluated using REDS status relative to the task target.

Absent:
  The target feature is not present.

Not Realized:
  The target feature is attempted but does not functionally materialize.
  - attempted but not functional
  - invoked but not developed
  - does not carry meaning
  - requires inference from the listener to complete

Developing:
  The target feature is functionally present.
  - functionally present
  - followable from what is explicitly stated
  - inconsistent
  - breaks under pressure or across parts of the response

Sustained:
  The target feature is controlled across the full response.
  - controlled
  - distributed across the response
  - stable under task demands

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SCORING ALIGNMENT — ILR 2 TARGET

Score 3 corresponds to Sustained performance.

Developing performance does not meet the threshold for Score 3.
Not Realized performance does not meet the threshold for Score 2.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

FUNCTIONAL THRESHOLD

A feature is considered functionally realized when it carries meaning in a way
that can be followed from what is explicitly stated, without requiring the
listener to infer missing steps, relationships, or structure.

If a feature is present in form but does not carry meaning (e.g., structures are
used but do not result in a usable, interpretable outcome), it is classified as
Not Realized.

4. SCAFFOLD ARCHITECTURE

The scaffold operates across three layers plus a prompt difficulty parameter set
at task design.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
LAYER 1 — Fixed Infrastructure — Always Active, Not Instructor-Configurable
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Past Tense Gate
  Regular past tense sustainment evaluated on every narration. Score cap rule
  never changes regardless of any instructor configuration. Cannot be disabled
  or adjusted.

Disfluency Default
  Disfluency monitored on every narration. Score cap applies only when numerous
  and meaning-impeding. This behavior is always active and is never overridden
  or replaced by any instructor configuration. Selecting Fluency Focus as a
  monitor increases feedback priority for disfluency patterns — it does not
  change monitoring behavior, scoring logic, or the cap threshold.

Reference Clarity
  Subject and referent tracking monitored on every narration. Always reported
  in feedback when breakdown occurs. This evaluation is always active regardless
  of instructor configuration. Selecting Reference Clarity as a Layer 3 monitor
  does not activate new criteria — it increases feedback prominence and reporting
  detail only.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
LAYER 2 — Primary Focus — Instructor Selects One
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Past Tense Control
  Feedback emphasis on tense usage. Increases feedback density and priority for
  tense-related issues. Does NOT modify the gate criterion or scoring logic.
  Re-evaluate in Phase 2 after observing whether gate failure already dominates
  feedback automatically.

Sequencing & Flow
  Focus on ability to move events forward in time with linking phrases and
  organizational devices. Levels: disconnected / linked / controlled.

Detail & Expansion
  Focus on adding information beyond simple event listing. Levels: bare listing /
  some elaboration / consistent expansion.

  NOTE: The Past Tense Control levels (Absent / Emerging / Sustained) are
  descriptive categories for feedback orientation only. They do not modify the
  gate criterion, alter scoring thresholds, or affect scoring outcomes in any way.
  The gate rule — regular past tense not sustained = Score 1 — applies on every
  narration regardless of which level is selected or whether Past Tense Control is
  selected as a primary focus at all. When the gate criterion fails, feedback will
  still prioritize past tense control regardless of primary focus selection.

  NOTE: Instructor selects one primary focus per task assignment. This selection
  drives feedback priority — the scorer leads with this dimension in all feedback
  output.

  Phase 2 decision checkpoint: If early deployment shows gate failure always
  dominates feedback automatically on Score 1 responses, Past Tense Control as a
  selectable focus may be redundant and can be retired.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
LAYER 3 — Optional Monitors — Instructor Selects Any Combination
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Basic Structures
  Monitors: past tense morphology, subject-verb agreement, basic word order,
  simple clause construction. These features are included because they directly
  support intelligibility at ILR 1-2 — errors in these areas impede comprehension
  of the narration. Article errors, preposition errors, and complex clause errors
  are excluded because they do not reliably impede comprehension at this level and
  are treated as ILR 3+ precision concerns outside the scope of this instrument.

Reference Clarity
  Reference clarity evaluation is always active as fixed infrastructure (see
  Layer 1). Selecting this monitor increases feedback prominence and reporting
  detail only. No new criteria are activated by this selection.

Fluency Focus
  Monitors false starts, repetition, and hesitation patterns. Shifts feedback
  priority toward disfluency patterns. Does NOT change the scoring cap rule —
  cap applies only when numerous and meaning-impeding regardless of this setting.

  NOTE: All three monitors are technically available for simultaneous selection.
  However, activating all three simultaneously may produce diffuse feedback that
  reduces instructional clarity. Recommended maximum: two active monitors per
  assignment. All three are permitted but not always optimal — evaluate
  multi-monitor feedback quality in Phase 2.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PROMPT DIFFICULTY — Set at Task Design Level — Not a Scaffold Category
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Topic Domain
  Controls lexical demand through prompt and topic selection. Routine personal
  events (daily life, familiar experiences) represent standard difficulty.
  Familiar public events (holidays, movies, common cultural references) and work
  tasks represent slight stretch. Lexical range is not a scaffold focus category
  and is not instructor-configurable within the scaffold.

5. CONFIGURATION CONSTRAINTS AND INTERACTION RULES

4.1 Hard Rules

- Layer 1 is always active. No instructor override.
- Instructor selects one and only one primary focus from Layer 2.
- Past Tense Control selection does not affect scoring or the gate. It increases
  feedback density and priority for tense-related issues only.
- Reference Clarity in Layer 3 increases reporting prominence — it is always
  monitored regardless of selection.

4.2 Recommended and Cautioned Pairings

  Sequencing & Flow + Detail & Expansion
    Status: Recommended
    Note: Most common classroom target

  Detail & Expansion + Fluency Focus
    Status: Recommended
    Note: Production load focus

  Sequencing & Flow + Lexical Domain (via prompt)
    Status: Recommended
    Note: Topic control focus

  Past Tense Control + any monitor
    Status: Permitted
    Note: Feedback density on tense + monitoring layer

  Lexical Domain (stretch) + Fluency Focus
    Status: Caution
    Note: Cognitive load warning displayed; diagnostic use only

  Cognitive load warning text (displayed at assignment creation):
  "High domain load and fluency monitoring are both active. Breakdown in this
  configuration may reflect domain difficulty rather than fluency control.
  Interpret fluency data cautiously."

6. SUBCATEGORY PROGRESSIONS BY LAYER 2 FOCUS

Each primary focus option maps to a three-level progression aligned with
ILR 1 -> 1+ -> 2 performance criteria.

6.1 Past Tense Control

  Absent
    Past tense not present or only isolated instances. Present tense dominant.
    Does not carry meaning as a past tense narration. ILR 1.

  Not Realized
    Past tense attempted but does not functionally materialize. Invoked in early
    moves but not sustained. Present tense intrusions are not patterned around
    specific move types — past tense is inconsistent across the full body of the
    narration and requires the listener to infer temporal relationships. ILR 1.

  Developing
    Past tense functionally present and followable from what is explicitly stated.
    Anchors narrative moves. Patterned inconsistency in descriptive or procedural
    sequences does not constitute gate failure — the feature is functionally
    present even if it breaks under pressure in specific move types. ILR 1+.

  Sustained
    Past tense controlled across the full narration. Distributed across all
    narrative move types. Stable under task demands. Minimal errors. ILR 2.

6.2 Sequencing & Flow

  Absent
    No sequencing markers or connective devices present. Events listed without
    order. Does not carry meaning as a connected sequence. ILR 1.

  Not Realized
    Sequencing attempted but does not functionally materialize. Markers invoked
    but do not organize events into a followable order. Listener must infer
    sequence from content rather than from explicit structure. ILR 1.

  Developing (Linked)
    Sequencing functionally present and followable from what is explicitly stated.
    Events connected with basic markers (first, then, after). Some organizational
    devices present. Breaks under pressure — sequence may be lost in extended
    portions but recovers. ILR 1+.

  Sustained (Controlled)
    Sequencing controlled across the full narration. Transitions distributed
    across all narrative moves. Referential continuity maintained and stable
    under task demands. ILR 2.

6.3 Detail & Expansion

  Absent
    Events named without any elaboration. No detail present beyond bare event
    labels. Does not carry meaning beyond a list. ILR 1.

  Not Realized
    Elaboration attempted but does not functionally materialize. Expansion
    invoked but does not develop — detail is present in form but does not add
    interpretable content. Listener must infer meaning. ILR 1.

  Developing (Some elaboration)
    Detail functionally present and followable. Student attempts to clarify or
    add context to some narrative moves. Expansion is inconsistent — present in
    some moves, absent in others. Breaks under pressure in extended portions.
    ILR 1+.

  Sustained (Consistent expansion)
    Supporting details present and distributed across the narration. Student
    extends most narrative moves. Stable under task demands, though not always
    able to make the message fully clear. ILR 2.

7. OPEN ITEMS AND PHASE 2 DECISIONS

7.1 Past Tense Control as Primary Focus — Phase 2 Checkpoint

  Retain in Layer 2 for MVP. After early deployment, evaluate:
  - Do instructors select it? Or ignore it?
  - Does selecting it produce visibly different feedback output from the default?
  - Do users report wanting denser tense feedback, or does the scorer already
    surface it sufficiently on Score 1?

  Decision rule: If redundant -> retire. If instructors use it meaningfully -> keep.

7.2 Monitor Cap

  No hard cap on simultaneous monitors in MVP. Evaluate in Phase 2:
  - Does activating all three monitors produce feedback that is too diffuse?
  - Should a soft limit of two monitors be enforced rather than recommended in
    instructor guidance?

7.3 Disfluency Cap — STT Pipeline

  Current implementation limitation: Disfluency evaluation is partial under
  transcript-only scoring conditions. Deepgram transcripts preserve false starts,
  filled pauses, and self-corrections, but do not capture silent pauses, delivery
  rate, or labored speech — all of which are criterion-level evidence at the
  ILR 1/2 boundary. As a result, certain disfluency patterns that would trigger
  the score cap may go undetected.

  Action required: Determine whether Deepgram timestamps, pause duration markers,
  or confidence scores can be passed to the Claude scorer as a supplementary
  metadata field alongside the raw transcript. Until resolved, the disfluency cap
  should be treated as partially implemented.
