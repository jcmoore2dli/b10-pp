The Three Asks — In Sequence

S1
Protected Development Time
Ask directed to: Supervisor · Supervisor can authorize directly
The ask
Four weeks of protected development time in the fall. No budget required. This is a scheduling request.
What it enables
Moving from Phase 2A (schema complete) to a working Phase 3 pilot with real students and real data. Without protected time, this happens across 18+ months of fragmented evenings. With it, it happens in one sustained block.
Risk to program
Zero. One instructor's schedule is adjusted for four weeks. The platform either works or it doesn't. Either way, nothing was spent.
When to ask
Now — or as soon as the April sprint produces visible results. Show, then ask.
Framing for the supervisor: "I'm not asking for money. I'm asking for time to build something that could change how this program operates — and the only cost right now is four weeks on my schedule."
S2
Purchase Card Authorization
Ask directed to: Budget authority · Carried there by the supervisor
The ask
Government purchase card authorization for API services. Annual cost at Phase 3: approximately $176. Well below the federal micro-purchase threshold of $10,000. No competitive bidding required.
What it covers
OpenAI Whisper transcription · Anthropic Claude scoring and feedback · Firebase cloud services · 50 students · full year of operation
Context
This is an operational cost, not a project investment. It is recurring, predictable, and smaller than a single textbook purchase per student per year.
When to ask
After the pilot produces data. The supervisor has already been briefed. Budget authority is not hearing this for the first time.
What the supervisor carries upward: "We have a working pilot, real student data, and an annual operating cost of $176 for 50 students. I am requesting purchase card authorization to continue operations."
S3
Formal Program Adoption
Ask directed to: Budget authority · Supported by pilot evidence
The ask
Formal recognition of B10-PP as a program resource. This may include protected development time on an ongoing basis, expanded API authorization as the platform scales, and institutional endorsement for broader deployment.
What makes it viable
By this point the platform is not a proposal. It is a working system with a documented pilot record, instructor feedback, student performance data, and a demonstrated cost structure. The decision is whether to scale something already working — not whether to fund something untested.
The cost at full scale
All phases · 50 students · ~$576/year · $11.52 per student per year
When to ask
After S2 is approved and the platform has operated through at least one full pilot cycle with documented outcomes.
The argument at this stage: "This platform encodes institutional knowledge — the B10 corpus, the MF lexicon, the scaffold framework — that cannot be replicated by a vendor without the domain expertise that built it. Scaling it is not a technology investment. It is a capacity preservation decision."

Meeting Guide — Where the Asks Happen

Meeting 1 — The Demonstration
With Supervisor
Timing
After April sprint produces visible results — ideally a working prototype with one real passage scored
Purpose
Show, don't tell. Demonstrate the student queue, the B10 ID lookup, one scored attempt with feedback. Let the platform speak.
What you bring
Ecosystem visual · UI mockups · Supervisor brief · A live demo if possible
The ask
None yet. The only ask is that they understand what this is. Plant the seed.
What you leave behind
The supervisor brief — one page, problem / solution / ask framing — so they can represent it accurately without you in the room
Meeting 2 — The Ask for Time (S1)
With Supervisor
Timing
Shortly after Meeting 1 — same month if possible. Strike while interest is fresh.
Purpose
Request four weeks of protected development time in the fall. Frame it as a scheduling decision, not a resource decision.
The ask
S1 — Protected time. "Four weeks in the fall. No budget. I do the work. At the end of it we have a pilot-ready platform."
Anticipated objection
"We can't spare you for four weeks." Response: "What if it took four weeks to build something that runs for years and costs $176 a year to operate?"
Leave behind
Cost-by-phase table. Roadmap v9.1. The numbers make the case quietly.
Meeting 3 — Pilot Results + Budget Ask (S2)
Supervisor Carries Upward
Timing
After the pilot — approximately 4–6 weeks of live student use. Real data in hand.
Purpose
Present pilot evidence to supervisor. Supervisor then carries S2 to budget authority.
What you bring
Pilot summary — attempt counts, score distributions, instructor feedback, session note examples. The platform working as designed.
The ask to supervisor
S2 via supervisor. "I need you to take this to whoever can authorize a purchase card. The number is $176 for the year. Here is everything you need to make that conversation."
What supervisor carries
"We have a working pilot, real data, and an annual cost of $176 for 50 students. I am requesting purchase card authorization to continue."
Meeting 4 — Program Adoption (S3)
With Budget Authority
Timing
After S2 is approved and platform has operated through at least one full cycle. Budget authority is not hearing this for the first time.
Purpose
Formal program recognition. Potentially expanded authorization, protected development time as an ongoing allocation, and endorsement for broader deployment.
The ask
S3 — Formal adoption. Scale something already working. Not fund something untested.
The core argument
This platform encodes institutional knowledge that cannot be replicated by a vendor. It exists because of domain expertise built over years at this institution. Scaling it is a capacity preservation decision.

How the Supervisor Can Champion This

A guide for the supervisor — if they ask "how can I help?"
1.
Protect the April sprint. The week of April 20 is the first critical development block. Shielding that week from competing duties costs nothing and directly accelerates the timeline.
2.
Identify who holds the purchase card. The S2 ask is $176/year. Knowing who can authorize that before the pilot ends means the ask can move immediately when the evidence is ready.
3.
Name two SLT instructors for the pilot. The platform was designed around the SLT workflow. A pilot without SLT instructors only validates half the system. One or two names is all that is needed.
4.
Frame it upward as a throughput argument. Not a technology project. Not an instructor's side endeavor. A direct response to the program's continuity and throughput challenge — built internally, at near-zero cost, by a subject matter expert.
5.
Ask for 30 minutes with budget authority after the pilot. Not a formal briefing. Not a presentation. Just 30 minutes to show what the data looks like. The supervisor gets the meeting. The evidence makes the case.
